Ontology Comparison — Human Vision vs Optimized Vision

Finding the missing layer between construction bridge and deconstruction reality

The Missing Layer: Intelligence Operations

Your ontology has two strong layers: Phases (chronological order) and Primitives (atomic physical actions bridged from construction). Between them sit 55 task codes — but these were built by reversing construction tasks. Construction doesn't have assessment, material routing decisions, intact recovery techniques, contamination gating, documentation/data capture, or the 45-minute problem-solving operations.

These aren't just "nice to have" — they're the operations that make deconstruction different from demolition and that create the value. A demolition crew doesn't grade lumber. A construction crew doesn't photograph what they just pulled for resale. The intelligence layer is what turns physical labor into data, materials into inventory, and a tear-down into a business.

The Key Insight

The construction bridge captured what hands do — pry, cut, carry, lift. But deconstruction's value comes from what the brain does — grade, assess, route, price, document.

Your 90/45 model already accounts for this — 90 minutes of body, 45 minutes of brain. But the ontology only codified the 90. The 19 new tasks codify the 45. Now the full shift is in the system.

This also means every intelligence operation is trackable — time per grade, pricing accuracy, routing decisions, photo coverage. When the robot tier arrives, these are the tasks humans keep longest (Tier 4+). They're also the tasks that train the AI. The intelligence layer is both the last to automate and the first to compound.

The Redundancy Problem

Once materials leave the site, ML Systems loses control. A board sold at BOH could be resold on Facebook Marketplace, listed on Craigslist, or flipped by a contractor — all without ML Systems attribution. Worse: without provenance tracking, the same material could theoretically be listed twice, sold to two buyers, or re-entered into the system as "new" recovered material.

The fix isn't just preventing fraud — it's branding the material itself so that every piece of recovered lumber, every salvaged cabinet, every concrete panel carries ML Systems provenance through its entire second life. The brand travels with the material, not just the receipt.

ML Provenance System — 3 Layers

1

Physical Brand Mark

On the Material Itself

Every graded piece of lumber gets an ML Systems brand stamp — a physical ink stamp or brand burned into the end grain. Includes:

  • ML mark — the logo, physically on the wood
  • Material ID — unique code (e.g. ML-2026-001-R042)
  • Grade — A/B/C stamped on the piece
  • QR code sticker — links to digital provenance record
  • Species & dimensions — stamped or tagged

Precedent: FSC-certified lumber carries a chain-of-custody stamp. ML Systems does the same for recovered materials.

2

Digital Provenance Record

QR → Full Material History

Every QR code links to a provenance page on mlsystemsri.com showing:

  • Source house — address, year built, era (tells buyer what adhesives/treatments to expect)
  • Recovery date — when it was pulled
  • Recovery method — which task code was used (links to ontology)
  • Grade + photos — condition at time of recovery
  • Contamination status — lead test result, moisture scan
  • Sale status — available / sold / reserved
  • ML Systems branding — "Recovered by ML Systems LLC"

Anti-redundancy: once marked SOLD, the QR page shows "This material has been purchased" — cannot be relisted.

3

Inventory Deduplication

System-Level Prevention

The Material ID is the primary key in the system. It prevents:

  • Double-listing — same ID can only have one active BOH listing
  • Double-sale — once sold, ID is locked to buyer
  • Ghost inventory — material must be graded + photographed before listing is created
  • Re-entry fraud — ID is tied to source project. Same board can't be "recovered" from two houses
  • Lifecycle tracking — recovered → graded → listed → sold → (optional: installed in next build → tracked in Design Studio)

Full circle: if the buyer uses the material in an ML Systems build, it re-enters the system with a new context — same ID, new lifecycle stage.

Material ID Format

ML-2026-001-R042
ML ML Systems — always prefixed. The brand is in the ID itself.
2026 Recovery year
001 Project number (sequential — first decon = 001)
R042 Item number within project (R = roof, W = walls, F = floors, N = foundation, K = kitchen, H = hardware). 042 = 42nd item recovered from this phase.

This format means: even if someone resells an ML Systems board on Craigslist, the buyer can scan the QR or look up ML-2026-001-R042 and find ML Systems. The brand is in the material, not just on the price tag. When someone sees "ML" stamped on reclaimed lumber at a job site two years later, they know where it came from.

The Brand Flywheel

Every material that leaves ML Systems carries the brand with it. Every QR scan is a touchpoint. Every resale — even by a third party — routes attention back to mlsystemsri.com.

Material recovered → ML stamp + QR → Sold at BOH → Buyer scans QR → Sees ML Systems provenance → Installs in new build → Design Studio tracks it → Next decon recovers it again

The material ID never dies. It accumulates lifecycle stages. A board with three lifecycle entries is proof that circular construction works — and ML Systems made it happen every time.
