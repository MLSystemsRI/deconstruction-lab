# Builder's Open House — Turn a House Into a Store in a Day

> Deconstruction Lab | ML Systems | Lucent Lens
> "The house doesn't go to the store. The house becomes the store."
>
> Concept: Deconstruct a home and stage every recovered material on-site in display-ready zones — in real time, as the crew works. By end of day, the lot is a walk-through building supply yard. Next morning, open the gates.

---

## The Idea

Every deconstruction produces $12K–$46K of recoverable material. Currently the plan is: stage materials → load on trailers → haul to various channels (ReStore, scrap yard, FB Marketplace, landscape yards).

**Problem:** Hauling costs time and money. Multiple trips, multiple buyers, multiple negotiations. Materials sit in your yard waiting for buyers. Value leaks.

**Flip it:** Leave everything on-site. Organize it as it comes out — not in random piles, but in intentional display zones. Price-tag during staging (crew marks as they sort). At the end of the decon day, you don't have a demolished lot with piles of debris. You have a **walk-through materials yard** with everything categorized, priced, and ready to sell.

Next morning: open the gates, let builders, contractors, DIYers, and the community walk through. Cash and carry. Everything goes. Whatever doesn't sell in the window → then haul the remainder to secondary channels.

**Lucent Lens:** "Come see what was inside the walls. Buy what you need. The house that stood here becomes the material for the next one."

## Site Layout — The Lot Is the Floor Plan

A typical 2,500 SF home sits on a 50'×100' to 75'×150' lot. The house footprint is ~1,250 SF (roughly 50'×25' for a two-story). The rest is yard.

**After the house is down, you have the entire lot** — driveway, yard, the foundation hole (fenced), and the former footprint. That's your store.

### Zone Placement Logic

| Zone | Position | Why There |
|---|---|---|
| **1: Kitchen** | Front-left, near entry | Highest-value item. Anchor display. People see it first. |
| **2: Lumber** | Front-right, near driveway | Heaviest repeat item. Contractors pull trucks up, load from here. |
| **3: Doors/Windows/Trim** | Mid-left | Browsable. People walk through, check sizes. |
| **4: Sheathing/Drywall** | Mid-right | Heavy, stackable. Near lumber for contractor one-stop. |
| **5: Flooring/Fixtures** | Lower-left | High-value display items. Hardwood bundles, bathroom fixtures. |
| **6: Hardware/Metals** | Lower-right | Folding table setup. Small items, sorted into bins. |
| **7: Concrete** | Back, around foundation hole | Heaviest material. Stays where it lands. Buyers bring trucks to rear. |
| **8: Shingles/Siding/Insulation** | Back corners | Bulk/low-value. Out of the way. Shingles may not sell (stockpile for grinder). |

## Chronological Build-Out — The Store Builds Itself

The crew isn't "setting up a store." They're deconstructing a house. But where they put each material is intentional. The staging plan IS the store layout. Zero extra labor.

### 6:00 AM — Soft Strip Begins, Zone 1 Opens First

**Phase A: Kitchen Cabinets → Zone 1**

| Time | Crew Action | What Goes to Zone 1 | Display Setup |
|---|---|---|---|
| 6:00 | Disconnect appliances, pull out | Stove, fridge, dishwasher, microwave → lined up along back of Zone 1 | Appliances upright, doors closed, power cords tucked |
| 6:20 | Remove countertops | Countertop sections → set on two sawhorses in Zone 1 | Flat, face up, visible. If granite: one piece = showpiece. |
| 6:40 | Remove upper cabinets | Uppers → set upright against a temporary frame or fence panel in Zone 1 | Doors facing out. Group by cabinet width. |
| 7:10 | Remove base cabinets | Bases → set upright in row in front of uppers | Same orientation as installed — buyers can see the set. |
| 7:30 | Remove island (if present) | Island → center of Zone 1 | Freestanding display piece. |
| 7:45 | Sink + faucet, hardware | Sink on countertop display, hardware in a bin on the countertop | Visible, touchable. |

**Zone 1 is fully stocked by ~8:00 AM.** It looks like a kitchen showroom in a salvage yard. Cabinets upright, countertop displayed, appliances behind. Price tags on each group.

> **Pricing note:** Kitchen set gets ONE price (whole set) and individual prices. "Complete set: $4,000. Individual cabinets: $75–$200 each. Countertop: $400. Stove: $150." A buyer who wants the set gets a deal. A buyer who needs two base cabinets can do that too.

### 8:00 AM — Trim & Electrical, Zones 3 + 5 + 6 Fill

**Phase B: Finish Trim → Zone 3**

| Time | Crew Action | What Goes to Zone 3 | Display Setup |
|---|---|---|---|
| 8:00 | Pull interior doors off hinges | Doors → lean against A-frame racks (two sawhorses with a 2×4 cross-beam) in Zone 3 | Face out, cardboard between. Tag size on each (2/0×6/8, 2/6×6/8, etc.) |
| 8:15 | Pull baseboards | Baseboard bundles (tape by room/length) → set on sawhorses in Zone 3 | Bundles labeled: "Oak baseboard, 3-1/4", 42 LF" |
| 8:30 | Pull window + door casing | Casing bundles → next to baseboards | Same labeling. |
| 8:45 | Pull crown molding | Crown bundles → on sawhorses | Fragile — handle carefully. Label profile if distinctive. |
| 9:00 | Pull exterior trim (rake, fascia, soffit, corner boards) | Exterior trim bundles → end of Zone 3 or leaned against fence | Separate from interior. Label "exterior." |
| 8:00–9:00 | Remove gutters + downspouts | Gutters → Zone 8 area (siding/misc) | Bundle with wire. Low value but someone wants them. |

**Phase C: Electrical & Plumbing → Zones 5 + 6**

| Time | Crew Action | Where It Goes | Display Setup |
|---|---|---|---|
| 9:00 | Remove light fixtures | Zone 5 (Fixtures) — set on ground cloth or table | Group: chandeliers, vanity lights, ceiling mounts. Working = tagged "tested working." |
| 9:15 | Remove ceiling fans | Zone 5 | Set upright, blades attached if possible. |
| 9:20 | Pull breakers from panel | Zone 6 (Hardware) — bin labeled "breakers" | Sorted by amp rating (15A, 20A, 30A, etc.) |
| 9:30 | Remove bathroom fixtures (sinks, toilets) | Zone 5 | Sinks face-up on ground. Toilets upright. |
| 9:45 | Remove faucets, valves | Zone 5 (with sinks) or Zone 6 (hardware table) | Bag small parts per fixture. |
| 9:45 | Pull copper wire | Zone 6 — copper bundles | Coiled, tagged by gauge. "12/2 NM-B, ~200 ft" |
| 10:00 | Cut + pull copper pipe | Zone 6 — copper bundles | Bundled by diameter. |
| 9:30 | Remove tub | Zone 5 — set upright against fence/wall | Cast iron = heavy. Tag weight for buyers. |
| 9:45 | Remove water heater | Zone 5 — near appliances or behind tub | Tag: gas/electric, gallon size, age if known. |
| 9:00 | Remove windows (from inside before wall lift) | Zone 3 — lined up in rows | Lean against A-frames. Tag size (3/0×4/0, etc.) and type (DH, casement). |

**By 10:00 AM — Zones 1, 3, 5, and 6 have inventory.** The "showroom" side of the lot (left side in the layout) is already stocked with finish materials, kitchen, fixtures, and windows — the stuff homeowners and small contractors want.

### 10:15 AM — Crane Starts, Structural Zones Fill

**Phase 1: Roof → Zones 2 + 4 + 8**

The crane lifts roof sections to the staging area. Ground crew disassembles each section. As materials separate, they go to their zones.

| Time | Crew Action | Material | Zone | Display Setup |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 11:00–12:30 | Ground crew pulls rafters off first section | Rafters (2×8, 2×10) | **Zone 2 (Lumber)** | Stack flat by dimension. Longest on bottom. Tags: "2×8×12, rafter-grade, qty 8" |
| 11:30 | Ridge board separated | Ridge board (2×10, 2×12) | Zone 2 | Set on top of rafter stack or separate. Tag. |
| 11:30 | Ridge beam (if present) | LVL or built-up beam | Zone 2 — **featured** | Prominent position. LVL beams are expensive new. |
| 11:15 | Collar ties, blocking removed | Small lumber | Zone 2 — separate stack | "Blocking/shorts" pile. Priced per piece or by the pile. |
| 11:30 | Sheathing pried off | OSB or plywood sheets | **Zone 4 (Sheathing)** | Stack flat. Separate OSB from plywood. Tag: "1/2" plywood, 4×8, qty 12" |
| 12:00 | Hardware collected | Hurricane ties, rafter clips, hangers | **Zone 6 (Hardware)** | Into bins on the folding table. Sort by type. |
| 11:00 | Shingles shoveled into pile | Shingle material | **Zone 8 (Shingles)** | Stockpile. NOT for open house sale — grinder feedstock. But tag it: "Shingle aggregate available, call for price" for paving contractors. |
| 12:00 | Fascia/rake boards (if not already in Zone 3) | Trim boards | Zone 3 | Add to exterior trim section. |
| 12:00 | Underlayment rolled | Synthetic underlayment | Zone 8 or trash | Only display if intact rolls. |

**By 12:30 — Zone 2 (Lumber) has its first inventory: rafters, ridge, blocking.** Zone 4 has roof sheathing. Zone 6 hardware table is getting populated.

**Phase 2: Walls → Zones 2 + 3 + 4 + 6**

Crane lifts wall sections to staging. Ground crew works them on their sides.

| Time | Crew Action | Material | Zone | Display Setup |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1:00–2:30 | Ground crew pulls drywall off wall sections | Drywall sheets (intact) | **Zone 4 (Drywall)** | Stack on pallets by size (4×8, 4×12) and thickness (1/2", 5/8"). Cover top with a sheet of plywood if rain risk. Tag: "Used drywall, 1/2" 4×8, nail holes only, qty 30" |
| 1:30 | Insulation pulled | Fiberglass batts | **Zone 8 (Insulation)** | Bag in clear trash bags or bundle with wrap. Tag: "R-13, 3-1/2" × 15", used" Low value — price to move. |
| 1:45 | Sheathing pried off | Exterior OSB/plywood | **Zone 4** | Add to sheathing stacks. Separate from roof sheathing if different thickness. |
| 2:00 | Siding removed | Vinyl, wood, or fiber cement | **Zone 8 (Siding)** | Bundle by type. Vinyl: nested, bundled. Wood: stacked, tagged by profile. |
| 1:30–2:30 | Studs separated | 2×4 or 2×6 studs | **Zone 2 (Lumber)** | Stack by dimension. Studs are the volume item — biggest stack in Zone 2. "2×4×92-5/8" precut studs, qty ~200" |
| 2:00 | Plates separated | Top/bottom plates | Zone 2 | Stack with studs or separate pile. Tag length. |
| 2:00 | Headers removed | 2×10, 2×12, LVL headers | Zone 2 — **featured** | These are bigger lumber. Premium stack. |
| 2:00 | Posts removed | 4×4, 4×6, 6×6 | Zone 2 — **featured** | Stand upright if possible or stack prominently. High value. |
| 2:00 | Shearwall holddowns unbolted | Simpson HDU, straps | **Zone 6 (Hardware)** | Bin labeled "holddowns." $25–$50 value each new — price at $10–$20 used. |
| 2:00 | Windows (if still in sections) | Windows | Zone 3 | Add to window row. |
| 2:30 | Exterior doors removed | Entry, sliding glass | Zone 3 | Lean against A-frames. Tag type and size. |

**By 2:30 — Zone 2 is heavy.** Studs, rafters, plates, headers, posts, ridge, beams. This is the contractor zone. Zone 4 has drywall on pallets + sheathing stacks. Zone 6 hardware table is full.

**Phase 2.5: Pull Hardwood (inside, before floor lift)**

| Time | Crew Action | Material | Zone |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2:30–3:00 | 2 crew members enter exposed floor from above, pull hardwood strips | Hardwood flooring bundles | **Zone 5 (Flooring)** — premium position |

**Phase 3: Floor System → Zones 2 + 4 + 6**

| Time | Crew Action | Material | Zone | Display Setup |

| 3:30–4:30 | Ground crew separates subfloor from joists | Subfloor sheets (3/4" T&G) | **Zone 4** | Stack with sheathing. Tag: "3/4 T&G subfloor, 4×8" — higher value than roof sheathing. |

| 4:00 | Bridging, blocking | Short 2× lumber + metal X-bridging | Zone 2 (shorts pile) + Zone 6 (metal bridging) | |
| 4:00 | Joist hangers, post caps | Connectors | **Zone 6** | Bins on table. |

**By 4:30 — Zone 2 (Lumber) is fully stocked.** Every piece of dimensional lumber from the entire house is sorted and stacked: studs, plates, rafters, joists, headers, beams, posts, rim, blocking. This is a mini lumber yard.

| 4:30–7:00 | Crane lifts pre-cut wall panels out of hole | Foundation wall panels (4'×8') | **Zone 7 (Concrete)** | Lean against each other in rows, like dominoes. Tag each: "8" poured concrete panel, 4'×8', 3,200 lbs, rebar intact." |

**By 7:00 — Zone 7 is the back-lot heavy yard.** Concrete panels leaned in rows, slab sections stacked, RCA pile, rebar bundles. This zone is for contractors with trucks and equipment — not the casual browser.

## The Open House Event

### When

**Option A — Next morning (recommended).** Decon Day 1 = teardown. Day 2 morning = open house. Gives time for social media push the evening before ("Tomorrow at 8 AM — everything must go").

**Option B — Same afternoon.** If soft strip was fast and structural work finished early, open the lot at 3–4 PM while ground crew is still finishing. Buyers browse the filled zones while crew stocks the rest. "Live deconstruction — watch it come apart, buy it on the spot."

**Option C — Weekend event.** Decon Mon–Tue, open house Saturday. Gives time for marketing, signage, and community outreach. Risk: materials sit 3–5 days (weather, theft, vandalism).

## Day-Of Flow — How the Open House Runs

### Setup (7:30–8:00 AM)

| Task | Detail |
|---|---|
| Unlock/untarp high-value zones | Kitchen, fixtures |
| Set out zone signs | Printed or hand-written, zip-tied to stakes |
| Post price sheet at entry | Full list, also available as photo/PDF for texting |
| Open payment station | Square reader on phone, cash box, receipt book |
| Brief any helpers | "Don't negotiate below [floor price]. Bundle deals are pre-approved. Call me for the whole-lot buyer." |

### During (8:00 AM–2:00 PM)

| Hour | Expected Flow |
|---|---|
| 8:00–9:00 | Contractors arrive first (they start early). Lumber zone, kitchen zone. Big-ticket purchases. |
| 9:00–11:00 | Steady flow. Mix of contractors and DIYers. Doors, windows, trim, fixtures. |
| 11:00–1:00 | Slows down. Casual browsers, neighbors. Small purchases. |

## The 4 Returns — Open House Edition

| Return | What | Value |
|---|---|---|
| **1. Material value** | Direct revenue from sales | $9,350–$25,400 |
| **2. Ontology data** | What sells, at what price, to whom, how fast. Per-item demand data. | Pricing model for every future house. |
| **3. Robot training signal** | Photos of every zone, every material sorted. Labeled dataset of building components post-disassembly. | Visual identification training. |
| **4. Market intelligence** | Who shows up. What they ask for. What they don't buy. What price moves them. | Demand mapping for secondary materials market. |

**The open house IS the market research.** You're not guessing what builders will pay for used floor joists. You're watching them buy (or not buy) used floor joists at $10 each, adjusting to $8, and logging the price point. Every open house trains the pricing model.

## Lucent Lens — Why This Matters

The secondary lumber market doesn't exist yet (First Principle #1: "awareness before infrastructure"). An open house creates it — one lot at a time.

A contractor who buys 50 floor joists at half price tells another contractor. A homeowner who gets a $20,000 kitchen for $4,000 tells their neighborhood. The curious neighbor who walks through sees what's inside their own walls for the first time.

**The house glows one more time before it's gone.** Every stud, every joist, every cabinet — visible, valued, and available. Not crushed in a dumpster. Not hidden in a landfill. Laid out in the sun with a price tag.

That's the Lucent Lens. The house helps humans one last time.
