### What the State Wants

From the Governor's office and Secretary Goddard's statements:

1. **Cost efficiency** — "Preference given to proposals demonstrating cost efficiency and minimized per-unit subsidy amounts"
2. **Innovative construction** — "Encourages innovative construction and design approaches, including factory-built housing and smaller-footprint homes"
3. **Lower development costs** — explicit scoring criterion
4. **Ready-to-go projects** — "meeting developers where they are"
5. **Owner-occupied, anti-speculation** — deed restrictions mandate occupancy, discourage speculative resale
6. **Speed** — construction must start within 9 months of award

### The 3 Barriers the State Identified (Housing 2030 Plan)

The full Housing 2030 plan (Dec 2025) identifies **three systemic obstacles** to housing production:

1. **Restrictive zoning** — only 20% of RI is zoned for two-family; 31 of 39 municipalities below the 10% affordable threshold
2. **Rising construction costs** — land values up 81% (2012–2022), materials and labor costs climbing
3. **Gaps in state and federal funding** — demand exceeds available subsidy

**ML Systems directly attacks barrier #2.** Deconstruction eliminates demo costs and provides recovered materials that reduce new construction hard costs. No other applicant is addressing construction costs from the material supply side.

### Scoring Strategy — Where Decon Wins Points

**Category 3 (TDC) is the big one — 12 points.**

This is where our operations score. TDC = Total Development Cost per affordable unit. Donated land doesn't count as a cost (good — if a municipality donates the lot, our TDC drops further).

| Scenario | TDC/Unit | Points | How |
|---|---|---|---|
| Conventional stick-built | $375K-$500K | 0-8 pts | High material + labor + demo costs |
| Factory module + conventional site work | $250K-$375K | 8-12 pts | Module saves on structure |
| **Factory module + decon site work (ML Systems)** | **$186K-$310K** | **12 pts** | Module + recovered materials for foundation, site work |
| **Stick-built + decon materials (ML Systems)** | **$220K-$350K** | **10-12 pts** | Recovered framing, sheathing, foundation reuse |

**At $186K-$310K TDC, we're firmly in the <$350K tier = 12 points.** Most conventional applicants will land in the $375K-$450K range = 3-8 points. We have a 4-9 point advantage on this category alone.

**Category 4 (Leveraging) is the other big one — 12 points.**

This measures how little grant money you need per unit. Lower ask = more points.

| Grant Ask/Unit | Points | What It Means |
|---|---|---|
| <$25K | **12 pts** | You barely need the grant — project is nearly self-funding |
| $25K-$40K | 9 pts | Strong leverage — other funding covers most costs |
| $40K-$55K | 7 pts | Good leverage |
| $55K-$70K | 5 pts | Moderate |
| $70K-$85K | 3 pts | Weak leverage |
| $85K-$100K | **0 pts** | Asking for the max — no bonus for this |

**The math:** If our TDC is $250K and sale price is $300K, the gap is only $0 — we might not even need the grant. But we DO want to ask for something (the grant covers cost overruns, provides a buffer, and the program's purpose is to subsidize). **Sweet spot: $25K-$40K/unit = 9 points.** That's still $125K-$200K for a 5-unit project.

Alternatively, if TDC is $310K and sale price is $300K, the gap is $10K — ask for $25K/unit to cover the gap plus developer fee = **12 points.**

**Compare: a conventional developer with TDC $425K and sale price $350K has a $75K gap. They ask for $75K-$100K/unit = 0-3 points.** We're 9-12 points ahead on leveraging.

**Combined advantage on Categories 3+4:** ML Systems scores 19-24 points. Conventional developer scores 3-11 points. **That's a 10-16 point gap** on two categories — in a 70-point system where 32 is the threshold.

### Category 5 Reality Check — Readiness to Proceed

The max 12 points requires **fully approved building permits AND a signed schedule of value from the GC.** That's extremely hard for Round 1 without sites already permitted.

**Realistic Round 1:** "In process of receiving approvals + strong community engagement" = **up to 4 points.** The Builder's Open House concept IS community engagement — show the plan, describe the decon process, get community input. If a municipality is excited and provides a letter, that helps the "likely to result in approvals" argument.

**Round 2 play:** If we start the process now, Round 2 (late spring/summer 2026) could have us at "final plan approval" = 9 points. Or even permits by then = 12 points.

### Category 8 — Deed Restriction (Take the 6 Free Points)

**Minimum 10-year restriction = 6 points. Longer restriction = FEWER points.**

The state is explicitly rewarding shorter deed restrictions. This makes sense — they want homes to eventually enter the regular market, building equity for the buyer. The 10-year minimum with state equity sharing (100% year 1, declining 10%/year to 0% at year 10) is the exact structure to use. Don't offer anything longer.

### Section (e) Resilience — The Hidden Decon Advantage

The RFP asks: *"What adaptation strategies (plans, policies, or projects) will be taken to address the impact of climate change?"*

This is not just about flood zones or energy efficiency. **Deconstruction IS climate adaptation:**

| Strategy | Climate Impact | How We Do It |
|---|---|---|
| **Material reuse** | Reduces embodied carbon (manufacturing new materials = emissions) | Recovered lumber, sheathing, hardware go into new build |
| **Landfill diversion** | 160-190 tons/home diverted from landfill per decon | Source-separated materials, on-site reuse |
| **Reduced trucking** | Fewer dump truck trips to C&D facilities | Materials stay on-site or go to open house nearby |
| **Concrete reuse as RCA** | Avoids quarrying new aggregate | Foundation panels + crushed concrete = base course |
| **Smaller footprint homes** | Less material, less energy, less land impact | 600-1,200 SF (RFP encouraged range) |

No other applicant will have a resilience answer that includes "we recover 80-90% of the existing structure's materials and reuse them in the new build." This is a differentiator on a question where most developers will write about energy-efficient windows.

### The Developer Profit Revelation (Q10)

This is the single most important answer: **"There is no minimum or maximum developer profit."**

The purchase price cap is $399,999. The TDC per unit is separate. The gap between total funding (grant + sales proceeds) and total expenses is developer profit.

```
EXAMPLE — ML Systems Unit Pro Forma:

  Grant award:                  $70,000
  Sale price (to buyer):       $350,000
  ────────────────────────────────────────
  Total funding:               $420,000

  Land / site acquisition:     −$60,000
  Decon of existing structure:  −$15,000  (self-performed, reimbursable)
  New construction (hard cost): −$250,000 (reduced by $8K–$12K from reuse)
  Soft costs:                   −$30,000
  ────────────────────────────────────────
  Total expenses:              −$355,000

  DEVELOPER PROFIT:             $65,000/unit
```

**Without decon reuse:** Hard costs are $262K instead of $250K. Profit drops to $53K. Or you need $82K in grant instead of $70K.

**The decon savings flow to TWO places:** lower subsidy ask (scoring advantage) AND higher developer margin. You decide the split.

## The Pitch: Deconstruction as a Cost-Efficiency Engine

### The Problem the State Is Solving

RI needs 15,000 new homes by 2030. Many of these sites have existing structures that need to come down first. Conventional demolition:

- Costs **$15,000–$30,000** per house (demo + disposal)
- Sends **100–200 tons** to landfill per house
- Recovers **$0** in material value
- The developer asks for MORE subsidy to cover demo costs

### What ML Systems Changes

| Line Item | Conventional Developer | ML Systems Developer |
|---|---|---|
| Demo/disposal cost | −$15,000 to −$30,000 | $0 (self-performed decon) |
| Material recovery revenue | $0 | +$12,000 to $46,000 |
| Material reuse in new build | $0 savings | +$5,000 to $15,000 (avoided material cost) |
| **Net cost swing** | | **$32,000–$91,000 per unit** |

**That swing means ML Systems needs less subsidy per unit.** If a conventional developer needs $100K/unit, ML Systems might need $60K–$80K. That's a more competitive application on the "minimized per-unit subsidy" criterion.

### Where Recovered Materials Go Into New Construction

| Recovered Material | New Build Use | Cost Avoided (per unit) |
|---|---|---|
| Floor joists (2×10, 2×12) | Framing — floor system | $500–$1,500 |
| Wall studs (2×4, 2×6) | Framing — walls | $400–$1,200 |
| Sheathing (plywood/OSB) | Wall + roof sheathing | $300–$800 |
| Subfloor (3/4" T&G) | Floor decking | $200–$500 |
| Headers, beams, posts | Structural members | $200–$600 |
| Foundation panels (hybrid reuse) | Foundation walls | $2,000–$5,000 |
| RCA (crushed concrete) | Base course, fill, drainage | $500–$1,500 |
| Hardware (holddowns, hangers, clips) | Connections | $50–$150 |
| Doors + windows (if code-eligible) | Interior doors, utility windows | $200–$500 |
| **Total avoided material cost** | | **$4,350–$11,750** |

**Not every material goes back in.** Code compliance limits some reuse (windows may not meet current energy code, drywall is cosmetically imperfect). But structural lumber, sheathing, concrete, and hardware are all code-legal for reuse if structurally sound.

## The 51% Resale Value — How the Grant Accelerates It

### Without the Grant

The 51% target requires selling recovered materials at market rate through multiple channels (open house, ReStore, FB Marketplace, scrap yards). This takes time — building buyer networks, hauling, listing, negotiating.

### With the Grant

**You are your own buyer.** The recovered materials go directly from the old house into the new one. No sales channel needed. No buyer negotiation. No hauling. The "resale" is internal — the value is captured as avoided cost in the new build.

| Scenario | Materials Recovered | Materials Reused in New Build | Materials Sold (open house + channels) | Total Value Captured |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **A: Decon only (no new build)** | $12K–$46K | $0 | $9K–$25K (after sell-through) | $9K–$25K |
| **B: Decon + new build (own use)** | $12K–$46K | $4K–$12K (avoided cost) | $5K–$15K (surplus sold) | $9K–$27K |
| **C: Decon + grant-funded new build** | $12K–$46K | $4K–$12K (avoided cost) | $5K–$15K (surplus sold) | **$9K–$27K + $100K subsidy** |

**Scenario C is the play.** The grant doesn't just fund the new build — it creates the demand signal for the recovered materials. You're not hoping someone buys 50 floor joists at the open house. You're putting 50 floor joists into the next house.

### Volume Acceleration

The program requires **5+ units minimum.** At 5 units:

| Metric | Per Unit | 5-Unit Project |
|---|---|---|
| Homes deconstructed | 1 | 5 |
| Materials recovered | $12K–$46K | $60K–$230K |
| Materials reused | $4K–$12K | $20K–$60K |
| Grant funding | Up to $100K | Up to $500K |
| Open houses | 1 | 5 (or 1 big consolidated one) |

**5 houses deconstructed = 5 open houses = 5 data points for the ontology = 5 demonstrations for the community.** The grant program forces you to scale to 5+, which is exactly what the open house model needs to build the buyer network.

**The MVE principle applies to Sal's life, not just the company.** Every dollar of overhead before revenue is cash burn that can kill a startup. Liability insurance is not a $200 fee — it's a significant annual commitment that becomes ongoing overhead the moment you activate it.

**The investor page ask must include this.** The capital raise isn't just for equipment or sites — it's for the insurance that unlocks the GC registration that unlocks the grant application that unlocks $500K–$4M in project funding. The insurance is the lever that moves everything.

### The Shingle Arm — Winter Revenue from Roofer Diversion

Every decon produces 1.5–2.25 tons of shingles (Phase 1). But that's just our own roofs. The real volume comes from **every roofer in Rhode Island.**

RI generates ~45,000–50,000 tons/year of tear-off shingles. All of it goes to landfill at $100/ton tipping. There are **zero** shingle recyclers in the state. Every roofer pays to throw shingles away.

**The play:** ML Systems operates an asphalt shingle grinder during winter months (when decon and construction slow down). Roofers divert their tear-offs to our site instead of the landfill. We charge less than the landfill tipping fee. They save money. We grind the shingles into Recycled Asphalt Shingles (RAS) and sell to hot-mix asphalt plants.

```
ROOFER (year-round, every roofer in RI)
     │
     │  Tear-off shingles → currently paying $100/ton to landfill
     │
     ▼
ML SYSTEMS STOCKPILE (site lease or owned yard)
     │
     │  Roofer pays us $50–$70/ton (they save $30–$50/ton vs landfill)
     │
     ▼
WINTER GRINDING (same crew, same site, off-season)
     │
     │  Grinder processes stockpile → RAS product
     │
     ▼
HMA PLANTS / PAVING CONTRACTORS
     │
     │  Buy RAS at $35–$50/ton (replaces virgin asphalt binder)
     │
     ▼
REVENUE: Tipping fee income + RAS sales − grinder lease − site cost
```

**Why this matters for the grant pitch:**

1. **Year-round revenue.** Decon + construction is seasonal. The shingle arm generates income in winter when the crew would otherwise be idle.
2. **Same equipment, same crew.** The grinder lease, the magnetic separator (Phase 1 + Phase 4 nails), the site — all shared assets across the platform.
3. **State benefits.** RI has 45K–50K tons/year going to landfill. RIRRC is over capacity. Every ton we divert is a ton the state doesn't have to manage. This aligns directly with the Resilience question in the RFP (Section e).
4. **Roofer relationships.** Every roofer who diverts to us becomes a contact who knows about our decon operations. They see tear-offs from their perspective — we see the whole house. When they're on a job and the homeowner asks "who can take this whole house down?" — we're the answer.

**This is not in the grant application pro forma** — it's a separate revenue arm. But it strengthens the narrative: ML Systems isn't a one-project developer asking for a grant. It's an **operating platform** with year-round revenue streams from material recovery, and the grant-funded homes are one output of that platform.
