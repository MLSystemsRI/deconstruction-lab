## People on Site

There are two kinds of people on a deconstruction site: **constants** and **shift workers.**

### Constants — On site all day, both days

| Person | Role | Why constant |
|---|---|---|
| **Sal** | Supervisor / Founder | Full awareness of every phase, every decision, every problem. The continuity. |
| **Crane Operator** | Crane | Crane is the clock. Operator needs to see the full picture — what's been cut, what's rigged, what's next. |
| **Brokk Operator** | Brokk 170 | Foundation prep starts Day 1 and runs underneath everything. Brokk keeps running through shift changes. |

Constants take breaks when they can — not on a fixed schedule. The crane op rests when rigging is being reset. The Brokk op pauses between cuts. **The right rhythm for constants will come from field testing, not from a document.**

**Sal's role during the 45s:** When the shift crew stops to think, Sal is the person they think WITH. The 45-minute windows are when Sal collects insights from the crew — what took too long, what broke, what's the better way. Sal synthesizes across shifts: a Shift 2 crew member notices that trim pulls faster starting from the bottom, not the top. Sal carries that to Shift 3. The operation gets smarter every 2.25 hours. This is what "utilize the person, not the process" means in practice.

### Shift Workers — 90/45 cadence

Everyone else works in **shifts.** One shift = one block:

```
┌─────────────┐  ┌───────────────┐
│   90 min    │  │    45 min     │  = 1 shift (2.25 hrs)
│   LABOR     │  │    SOLVE      │
│             │  │               │
│  sustained  │  │  step back.   │
│  physical   │  │  think.       │
│  work       │  │               │
│             │  │  what took    │
│             │  │  too long?    │
│             │  │  what broke?  │
│             │  │  what's the   │
│             │  │  better way?  │
└─────────────┘  └───────────────┘
```

### The 45 Is Not a Break — It's the Other Half of the Job

A normal workday is 8 hours of continuous labor. People get tired around hour 5. They slow down. They repeat mistakes. They do things the hard way because they never stop to ask if there's a better way. By hour 7 they're running at 60% and making errors that cost time tomorrow.

**ML Systems doesn't work that way.**

You do 90 minutes of sustained physical work. Then you stop. You step back. You look at what you just did and ask:

- **What took too long?** Was there a faster sequence? Did I fight a nail that should have been cut? Did I carry material 50 feet when the staging zone should have been closer?
- **What broke?** Did I damage a piece of trim trying to save it? Why? Was the tool wrong, the angle wrong, or the method wrong?
- **What's the better way?** If I had to do that exact 90 minutes again, what would I change? Tell Sal. Tell the crew. That insight is worth more than another 90 minutes of grinding.
- **What did I learn about this house?** What surprised me? What was the trim species? How was the kitchen attached? That knowledge goes into the platform and makes the next house faster.

Then you go back in for the next 90 minutes — **with the insight.** You don't repeat the same mistakes. You adjust. Every cycle gets sharper.

**This is why ML Systems pays for both.** You're not being paid for 90 minutes of labor and 45 minutes of sitting around. You're being paid for 90 minutes of labor and 45 minutes of thinking about how to do it better. The labor is the body. The 45 is the brain. Both are compensated.

### The 2× Claim

A conventional crew works 8 hours and produces 6 hours of effective output (fatigue, lunch, slowdowns, repeated mistakes).

An ML Systems crew works 6 shifts (13.5 hrs on site) and produces:
- **9 hours of focused, rested 90-minute labor blocks** (no one is tired — you just had 45 minutes off)
- **4.5 hours of problem-solving** that makes each block faster than the last
- **Data capture** during the 45s that feeds BOH, Decon Lab, Design Studio — revenue and intelligence generated during the "break"

If each 90-minute block is even 15% more efficient than continuous fatigued labor (a conservative estimate), and the problem-solving compounds across shifts, **you get more done in 2 days with this structure than a conventional crew gets in 4.**

The person who works 3 shifts isn't doing 4.5 hours of work. They're doing 4.5 hours of work *plus* 2.25 hours of making the operation smarter. That's the value. That's what the pay is for.

### How Shift Workers Use the Structure

A shift worker picks which shifts they show up for:
- Work 1 shift (2.25 hrs) and go home
- Work 3 shifts across the day
- Work all 6 shifts if you're built like that
- Come back Day 2 for different shifts

**Nobody is required to work consecutive shifts.** Pick the ones that fit your schedule or your skills.

During the 45, you might:
- **Problem-solve** — the primary purpose. Talk to Sal, talk to the crew, figure out a better approach for the next block.
- **Photograph and list materials** — the thing you just pulled is fresh in your hands. You know its condition. Put it on BOH.
- **Log data** — separation times, damage rates, material observations for Decon Lab.
- **Rest** — genuinely rest. A rested worker in the next 90 is worth more than a tired one grinding through.
- **Help plan** — look at the staging area, see what's piling up, suggest a better flow.
- **Run an errand** — get ice, grab supplies, pick up a tool that's needed.

**All of these are valuable.** But the one that pays for itself fastest is the problem-solving. One insight that saves 10 minutes in the next block, repeated across 12 shifts and every future house, is worth more than any single piece of trim.

## The Rule: Soft Strip BEFORE Structure

Construction finishes interior last. Deconstruction strips interior FIRST.

```
SOFT STRIP (no crane needed)          STRUCTURAL (crane is the clock)
──────────────────────────────        ──────────────────────────────
Phase A: Kitchen cabinets             Phase 1: Roof off
Phase B: Finish trim (int + ext)      Phase 2: Walls on sides
Phase C: Electrical & plumbing        Phase 3: Floor system out
                                      Phase 4: Foundation
```

**Why this order:** Everything inside the walls has value. Once the crane lifts a wall section and the ground crew starts disassembly, trim/cabinets/wiring are in the way and will be damaged. Get them out first. The soft strip crew works inside while the crane crew sets up outside.

### Can It Work? — Capacity Check

**Labor available:**

| Source | Day 1 | Day 2 | Total |
|---|---|---|---|
| Shift workers (avg 3.7/shift × 90 min) | 6 shifts = 33 person-hrs | 6 shifts = 30 person-hrs | **63 person-hrs** |
| Sal (13.5 hrs/day) | 13.5 hrs | 13.5 hrs | **27 hrs** |
| Crane operator (13.5 hrs/day) | 13.5 hrs | 13.5 hrs | **27 hrs** |
| Brokk operator (13.5 hrs/day) | 13.5 hrs | 10 hrs (done by Shift 11) | **23.5 hrs** |
| **Total** | | | **~140 person-hrs** |

**Original 1-day model:** 7 people × 14 hours = 98 person-hours.
**This model:** ~140 person-hours across 2 days. **42% more capacity**, spread out so nobody burns out.

**Crane time:**
- Crane runs Shifts 3–6 (Day 1) + Shifts 7–10 (Day 2) = 8 shifts × 90 min = **12 hrs of crane windows**
- Original model needed ~12–14 hrs. **Fits.**

**Foundation bottleneck — solved:**
- Brokk starts Shift 1, Day 1 (5:30 AM). Foundation panel lifts don't start until Shift 9, Day 2 (10:00 AM).
- That's **28.5 hours of elapsed time** for Brokk prep before the first foundation crane lift.
- Original model had 4.5 hours of head start. This has **6× more runway.**

### What Needs Field Testing

This model is a framework, not a script. Field testing will answer:

- **Do the constants actually need fixed breaks?** Or do they naturally pause during rigging resets, shift transitions, and equipment moves?
- **Is the universal 45-minute break too long? Too short?** Maybe some shifts it's 30, some it's 60.
- **Can phases really fit within 90-minute labor blocks?** Kitchen alone is estimated at 1.5–2.5 hrs. It might spill into Shift 2.
- **Does the crane actually stop during break?** If a section is mid-rig, you finish the lift. The break starts after.
- **How does the 2-day crane sequence work?** Crane shutting down end of Day 1, restarting Day 2 — does that lose time repositioning?
- **Right number of shifts per day?** 6 might be too many. 5 might be enough. The answer comes from the field.
- **Shift crew continuity** — does it matter if a different person shows up for Shift 5 than Shift 4? Or does the presence of Sal + crane op as constants make that fine?

**The document models it. The field proves it.**

## Phase C: Electrical & Plumbing Harvest

> Copper is the prize. Wire, pipe, and fixtures all have scrap or resale value. This phase also removes hazards before structural work begins.

### IN

| Input | Detail |
|---|---|
| Crew | 2 people (one with electrical knowledge) |
| Time | 2–3 hrs |
| Tools | Wire strippers, linesman pliers, pipe wrench, pipe cutter, reciprocating saw, voltage tester |
| Safety | **POWER MUST BE OFF AND LOCKED OUT.** Verify dead at every box. Water main shut off. |

### Electrical Sequence

| Step | Component | Method | Time |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Verify power off | Main breaker OFF, lock-out tag-out, verify dead with non-contact tester at multiple locations | 5 min |

| 3 | Remove outlets + switches | Pull from boxes. Not worth reselling individually — scrap the plastic, save covers if decorative. | 15–20 min |
| 4 | Remove breaker panel | Pull breakers (resalable if modern), disconnect feeders. Panel box = scrap. | 15–20 min |
| 5 | Pull wire | Cut at each box location. Pull wire through holes in framing. Bundle by gauge. | 45–60 min |
| 6 | Remove junction boxes | Pry from framing. Steel boxes = scrap. Plastic = trash. | 10 min |

### Plumbing Sequence

| Step | Component | Method | Time |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Verify water off | Main valve OFF. Open lowest fixture to drain. | 5 min |
| 2 | Remove fixtures (sinks, toilets, tubs) | Disconnect supply + drain. Unbolt. 2-person lift for tubs/toilets. | 30–45 min |
| 3 | Remove faucets + valves | Pipe wrench, basin wrench for faucets. | 15 min |
| 4 | Cut and pull copper pipe | Pipe cutter at accessible points. Pull sections through framing. Bundle. | 30–45 min |
| 5 | Remove water heater | Disconnect gas/electric + water lines. Dolly out. | 10–15 min |
| 6 | PEX / CPVC (if present) | Low scrap value. Pull if easy, otherwise leave — comes out with wall sections. | 10 min |

### Shearwall Holddowns — Why They Matter

Simpson HDU holddowns are $25–$50 each new. They're bolted to the foundation and nailed/screwed to the stud — not destroyed during wall lifting if the crane cut is above the bottom plate. Remove with a wrench after wall section is on its side. Reusable if not deformed.

### Posts

Carrying posts (4×4, 4×6, 6×6) are high-value lumber — long, straight, and structural-grade. Often Douglas Fir or Southern Yellow Pine. They carry beam loads down to the foundation. Identify them during pre-work structural assessment so the crane lift doesn't snap them.

### Floor Beams — The Crane Moment

Floor beams (LVL, steel W-flange, or 3-ply 2×12) span the basement/crawl and carry all joist loads. They're the heaviest single members in the house above the foundation.

## Phase 4: Foundation (Crane + Brokk)

> The heaviest phase. 160–190 tons of concrete. Brokk is the primary tool inside the hole.
> See `phase-4-foundation/analysis.md` for full detail.

### IN

| Input | Detail |
|---|---|
| Crew | 3 active (crane op + rigger + Brokk op), 4 finishing structural disassembly in staging |
| Time | 4–6 hrs (THIS IS THE BOTTLENECK — see note below) |
| Equipment | Crane, Brokk 170, concrete chain saw, core drill, rigging hardware |

### Sequence

1. Saw-cut foundation walls into 4'×8' panels
2. Core drill lifting holes
3. Brokk frees panels at base (breaks footing bond)
4. Crane lifts panels out → staging/flatbed
5. Walk-behind saw cuts slab into 4'×6' sections
6. Brokk loosens slab sections
7. Crane lifts slab sections
8. Brokk breaks footings, crane lifts chunks
9. Brokk cleans hole, crane lifts Brokk out (last lift)

|---|---|---|---|

| **Phase 4 Total** | | | **$4,480–$13,420** |

### The 1-Day Bottleneck

Phase 4 protocol says "2-day crane target" and "timeline is NOT solved." For 1-day logistics:

**The problem:** 50 wall panels × 15–20 min/panel = 12–17 hrs of crane time for walls alone. That's impossible in 1 day after doing Phases 1–3.

**The fix:** Brokk pre-frees panels during Phases 1–3. By the time the crane gets to Phase 4:
- Panels are already saw-cut and freed at the base
- Crane does quick picks: rig → lift → set = 3–4 min/panel
- 50 panels × 4 min = ~3.3 hrs
- Slab sections: similar speed = ~1.5 hrs
- Footings: Brokk crushes during slab lifts = ~1 hr
- **Total Phase 4 crane time: ~6 hrs** (tight but possible)

**Reality check:** Foundation in 1 day is aggressive. More realistic: soft strip + Phases 1–3 in Day 1, Phase 4 in Day 2. But the economics and logistics should be designed for 1-day target with Day 2 as overflow.

## Risk Register — 1-Day Target

| Risk | Impact | Mitigation |
|---|---|---|
| Foundation takes too long | Pushes into Day 2 evening | Brokk starts Day 1 Shift 1. 28+ hours of head start before first crane pick (Shift 9). |
| Rain/wind (>20 mph) | Crane lifts suspended | Check forecast 48 hrs ahead. Reschedule if >30% chance. 2-day model gives weather buffer. |
| Asbestos / lead discovered during soft strip | Stop work, abatement required | Pre-demo hazmat survey (required by RI law) |
| Kitchen cabinets are built-in / custom | Harder to remove intact | Pre-assess. May need Shift 1 + part of Shift 2. |
| Hardwood is glued down (not nailed) | Low recovery, skip | Test one board first. If glued, leave for wall section → ground crew |
| Shift worker fatigue | **Mitigated by 90/45 cadence** | No shift worker does more than 90 min sustained labor without a 45-min break. Pick your shifts. |
| Constants fatigue (Sal, crane op, Brokk op) | Safety risk, decision fatigue | **Needs field testing.** Constants take breaks opportunistically. 13.5-hr days are real. Monitor and adjust. |
| Phase spills across shifts | A phase doesn't finish in one 90-min block | Expected and fine. Sal + crane op are the continuity. New shift crew picks up where the last one left off. |
| Shift crew shows up with no context | Wastes first 10 min figuring out what to do | Sal briefs every arriving shift crew. Constants ARE the handoff — no written notes needed. |
| Staging area overflow | Sections pile up, crew falls behind | Pre-mark staging zones. 2-day model = more ground-crew shifts for disassembly. |
| Phone/connectivity issues | Can't upload photos or create listings | Pre-download BOH app. Offline photo capture → batch upload later. |
| Not enough shift workers sign up | Not enough hands for a shift | Sal + crane op + Brokk op can run light shifts (3 constants + 1–2 shift workers). Slow but possible. |

## Data Capture (Ontology Feed) — All Phases

> **90/45 integration:** Data capture is no longer a post-day task or a supervisor-only job. Every crew member captures data during every 45-minute data block. The phone is the instrument.

| Data Point | Captured During | By Whom | Ontology Use |
|---|---|---|---|
| Kitchen cabinet brand/condition/count | Shift 1 break | Whoever pulled them | Resale price prediction |
| Trim species and linear footage | Shift 2 break | Whoever pulled them | Old-growth identification, pricing |
| Copper weight per home | Shift 2 break | E&P crew | Scrap value forecasting |
| Roof section actual weights | Shift 3 break | Ground crew | Weight prediction model |
| Ring-shank damage rate | Shift 3–4 break | Ground crew | Sheathing recovery prediction |
| Wall section counts and sizes | Shift 5 break | Ground crew | Section planning optimization |
| Hardwood species and condition | Shift 7 break | Whoever pulled it | Flooring market pricing |
| Floor beam types (LVL vs steel vs built-up) | Shift 8 break | Ground crew | Structural assessment model |
| Concrete panel integrity rate | Shift 9 break | Brokk op / rigger | Reuse viability prediction |
| Brokk throughput (yd³/hr actual) | Shift 10 break | Brokk op | Equipment utilization model |
| Total crew hours per phase | Every break | Sal (running total) | Labor cost model |
| Photos (before/during/after each phase) | Every break | Shift crew (if they choose) | Visual ontology training |
| BOH listings created | Every break | Shift crew (if they choose) | Marketplace velocity model |
| Weather conditions | Shift 1 | Sal | Seasonal adjustment factors |

**Lucent Lens:** The person who pulled the trim is the person who knows how it was attached, what went wrong, and how to do it faster next time. That knowledge — shared during the 45, captured in the platform, carried by Sal to the next shift and the next house — is the compound interest of this operation. We pay for it because it's worth more than the trim itself.
