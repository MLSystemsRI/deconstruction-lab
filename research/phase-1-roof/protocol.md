# Phase 1: Roof Deconstruction Protocol

> Deconstruction Lab | ML Systems | Lucent Lens
> Target: 2,500 SF home, crane + 5-person crew, safety-first
> Goal: Roof off and materials sorted in **Day 1** of a 2-day full-house decon

---

## Why the Roof Is Phase 1

Construction builds bottom-up. Deconstruction works top-down. The roof is the most exposed, most weather-dependent, highest-risk assembly. Get it off first, then work downward in controlled conditions.

**The conventional approach:** 6–8 roofers strip shingles by hand, trash everything into a dumpster, then a demo crew tears off sheathing. Shingles go to landfill ($100.20/ton in RI). Sheathing is destroyed by ring-shank nails during pull. Total material value recovered: $0.

**Our approach:** Use the crane to lift roof sections intact. Flip on the ground. Disassemble in reverse construction order. Recover everything.

## Pre-Work (Before Day 1)

### Structural Assessment (1–2 hours, day before)

| Check | What to Look For | Action |
|---|---|---|
| Roof-to-wall connection | Hurricane ties? Toenails only? | Determines cut sequence |
| Load-bearing interior walls | Which walls support ridge/rafters? | Must brace BEFORE lifting |
| Ceiling joists vs. rafters | Are they separate or truss system? | Truss = lift whole; stick-frame = cut at rafter lines |
| Chimney / plumbing vents | Masonry chimney needs separate lift | Disconnect flashing, mark cut-around |
| Roof pitch | Steeper = harder to flip safely | >8/12 needs modified rigging |
| Sheathing condition | Rotted sections = structural weakness during lift | Reinforce or cut around |
| Electrical | Any roof-mounted panels, antenna, conduit | Disconnect/remove first |

### Bracing the Top Floor

**Critical safety step.** Removing the roof removes the diaphragm that braces the top-floor walls against racking. Without the roof, exterior walls can bow outward.

| Brace Type | Where | Material |
|---|---|---|
| Horizontal kickers | Corner to corner across attic/top floor | 2×6, temporary screwed |
| Diagonal bracing | Every 8' along exterior walls, top plate to floor | 2×4 diagonal |
| Window/door headers | Ensure headers above openings are not roof-dependent | Check before cutting |

**Rule:** Brace goes in BEFORE any roof cuts are made. Brace comes out LAST (during wall phase).

## The Lift Sequence

### Step 1: Cut Roof into Sections (45–90 min)

A 2,500 SF home typically has a 1,250–1,400 SF roof footprint (assuming ~2,500 SF of roof surface area on a gable roof).

**Section sizing — target 800–1,200 lbs per lift:**

| Roof Component | Weight per SF | For a 300 SF section |
|---|---|---|
| Shingles (architectural) | 2.5–4.0 lbs/SF | 750–1,200 lbs |
| Sheathing (1/2" OSB/plywood) | 1.5–1.8 lbs/SF | 450–540 lbs |
| Underlayment/ice shield | 0.3–0.5 lbs/SF | 90–150 lbs |
| Rafters (2×8 @ 16" OC) | 1.5–2.5 lbs/SF | 450–750 lbs |
| **Total per section** | **5.8–8.8 lbs/SF** | **1,740–2,640 lbs** |

**Recommended: 4–6 sections for a 2,500 SF roof.**

| Section | Cut Line | Approx Size | Weight |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Left gable to first interior wall | ~250–350 SF | ~1,500–2,500 lbs |
| 2 | First interior wall to ridge center | ~250–350 SF | ~1,500–2,500 lbs |
| 3 | Ridge center to next interior wall | ~250–350 SF | ~1,500–2,500 lbs |
| 4 | Next interior wall to right gable | ~250–350 SF | ~1,500–2,500 lbs |
| 5–6 | Dormers, extensions, or hip returns | Varies | Lighter |

**Cut order:**
1. Cut ridge first (reciprocating saw from ridge cap down through ridge board)
2. Cut along rafter lines (not between rafters — cut AT a rafter so each section has edge rafters for structural integrity during lift)
3. Cut rafter-to-plate connections last (this is the release cut — section is now free)
4. Rigger attaches slings BEFORE final release cut

### Step 2: Rig and Lift (15–20 min per section)

**Rigging method: Basket sling with spreader bar**

```
        CRANE HOOK
            │
      ┌─────┴─────┐
      │ SPREADER   │ (8–12' steel or aluminum bar)
      │    BAR     │
      └──┬─────┬──┘
         │     │
    SLING│     │SLING  (nylon, 2" wide, rated 5,000+ lbs)
         │     │
    ═════╧═════╧═════
    │   ROOF SECTION  │  (shingles facing UP during lift)
    │   rafters below │
    ═════════════════
```

| Safety Check | Requirement |
|---|---|
| Sling capacity | 2× section weight minimum |
| Tag line | Attached to far end — controls swing during lift |
| Clear zone | 1.5× boom length radius — no personnel |
| Wind | <20 mph for roof sections (large sail area) |
| Signal | Rigger is ONLY person communicating with operator |
| Lift height | Just enough to clear walls + swing to landing zone |

### Step 3: Flip Section on Ground (5–10 min per section)

**Why flip?** Shingles are the outermost layer (applied last in construction). To disassemble in reverse order, shingles need to be on top / accessible. Flipping also puts rafters UP — easier to cut nails and separate.

**Flip method:**
1. Crane sets section on edge (one long side on ground)
2. Ground crew guides as crane slowly rolls section over
3. Section lands shingle-side DOWN, rafters UP
4. — OR — if section is manageable, crew manually flips after crane sets it down

**Landing zone:** Prepared area with ground protection (plywood sheets or existing shingle stockpile as cushion). 20' × 30' minimum clear space per section.

### Step 4: Ground Disassembly — Reverse Order (20–30 min per section)

**Construction order:** Rafters → Sheathing → Underlayment → Shingles
**Deconstruction order (reverse):** Rafters → Sheathing → Underlayment → Shingles

Wait — after the flip, rafters are on top. So the sequence from top of flipped section down:

| Order | Layer | Method | Recovery |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Rafters** | Cut nails at sheathing connection (oscillating tool or recip saw flush-cut). Lift rafters off. | 80–95% recoverable as lumber (if not split). Stack by length. |
| 2 | **Sheathing (OSB/plywood)** | Pry from underlayment. Ring-shank nails are the enemy — use flat pry bar from edges. | 40–70% recoverable if careful. OSB worse than plywood. Ring-shanks cause splits. |
| 3 | **Underlayment / Ice & Water Shield** | Peel. Synthetic underlayment peels clean. Ice shield is self-adhered — scrape with flat shovel. | Underlayment: reusable if intact. Ice shield: waste (adhered to sheathing). |
| 4 | **Shingles** | Already on bottom (ground contact). Scoop/shovel into stockpile. | 100% recovered → grinder or on-site reuse |

### Ring-Shank Nail Problem

Ring-shank nails are designed to NOT come out. They're the #1 source of sheathing damage during decon.

| Strategy | How | Damage Level |
|---|---|---|
| Cut flush (best) | Oscillating multi-tool or recip saw with metal blade, cut nail between rafter and sheathing | Minimal — nail stub stays in sheathing, can be driven flush |
| Pull through sheathing | Cat's paw from rafter side, pull nail through | Moderate — 1/4" hole in sheathing (patchable) |
| Drive through | Punch nail through sheathing from rafter side | Moderate — clean hole, rafter intact |
| Pry sheathing off (worst) | Force | High — splits OSB, cracks plywood |

**MVE call:** Cut flush with oscillating tool. Saves the rafter AND the sheathing. Adds 2–3 min per sheet but preserves $15–25 of material per sheet.

## Safety Protocol

### Fall Protection (until roof is fully removed)

| Requirement | Spec |
|---|---|
| Harness + lanyard | Every person on roof, 100% tie-off |
| Anchor points | Ridge anchor or temporary anchor (OSHA 5,000 lb rated) |
| Perimeter | No one within 6' of unprotected edge without tie-off |
| Hole covers | Any penetration > 2" gets plywood cover, marked "HOLE" |

### Crane Operations

| Requirement | Spec |
|---|---|
| Lift plan | Written, reviewed by operator + rigger before first lift |
| Load chart | Posted in cab, checked against section weights |
| Outrigger pads | Full extension, on firm ground or cribbing |
| Swing radius | Barricaded — no personnel inside swing zone |
| Power lines | 20' minimum clearance (10' if <50kV, but 20' is standard) |
| Wind limit | Suspend lifts >20 mph (roof sections = high sail area) |

### Ground Disassembly

| Hazard | Control |
|---|---|
| Nails (stepping on) | Magnetic sweep every 30 min, nail aprons on crew |
| Silica (cutting shingles/sheathing) | Wet cut when possible, N95 minimum |
| Noise (saws, impacts) | Hearing protection above 85 dB |
| Ergonomics | Rotate between cutting and carrying every 45 min |
| Debris falling from flipped sections | Hard hats mandatory, stand clear during flip |

Every Phase 1 execution generates training data for the Decon Lab ontology:

| Data Point | Capture Method | Ontology Use |
|---|---|---|
| Section weights (actual vs estimated) | Crane load indicator readout | Weight prediction model |
| Cut time per section | Stopwatch / crew log | Task duration training |
| Ring-shank damage rate | Count damaged vs intact sheets | Material recovery prediction |
| Rafter recovery rate | Count intact vs split/damaged | Lumber grade prediction |
| Shingle tonnage (actual) | Scale at stockpile or crane weigh | Volume forecasting |
| Weather conditions | Log temp, wind, precip | Seasonal adjustment factors |
| Crew time per layer | Task log | Labor cost model |
| Photos (before/during/after) | Phone camera, timestamped | Visual ontology training |

**Lucent Lens integration:** Crew members logging data earn skill-tracking credit. Their experience becomes part of the ontology — their knowledge is valued, not just their labor.

## Connection to Shingle Arm

Once the roof is off and shingles are separated:

1. **Own shingles → stockpile** at build site
2. **Roofer shingles → stockpile** at same site (lease-structure model)
3. **Grinder processes stockpile** during downtime or batch schedule
4. **RAS product** → sell to HMA plants or reuse on site

Phase 1 roof decon feeds the shingle arm automatically. The roof you're taking apart generates feedstock for the processing operation that pays for your equipment.
