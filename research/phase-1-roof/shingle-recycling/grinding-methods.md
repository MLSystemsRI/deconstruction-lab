# Shingle Grinding to 3/8"–1/2" Minus — Full Spectrum Analysis

> Phase 1: Roof | Shingle Recycling | Deconstruction Lab
> Question: What's the MVE path to get tear-off shingles to HMA-plant spec?

---

## Why Shingles Are Hard to Grind

1. **Asphalt is thermoplastic** — above ~70–80F it softens, gums up, sticks to blades/screens/walls
2. **Granule surface is extremely abrasive** — ceramic granules wear through cutting edges 3–10x faster than wood
3. **Fiberglass mat resists tearing** — wraps around edges instead of shearing
4. **Nails/staples** in tear-offs will destroy any equipment without magnetic separation upstream
5. **Cold = your friend** — below ~40F asphalt becomes brittle and shatters. 70% increase in cracking documented by NRCA in freezing conditions

**Rhode Island winters (15–35F, Dec–Feb) are a free competitive advantage.**

## Critical Technical Factors

### Temperature = Everything

| Temp Range | Shingle Behavior | Grinding Quality |
|---|---|---|
| **Below 20F** | Very brittle, shatters on impact | Excellent — maximum throughput, minimum wear |
| **20–40F** | Brittle, granules shed easily | Great — ideal operating window |
| **40–70F** | Transitional | Acceptable with water spray |
| **Above 80F** | Soft, gummy, sticks to everything | Poor — screens plug, wear increases, production drops |

### Anti-Clog Techniques
1. **Water spray injection** — purpose-built grinders have it; can retrofit on others
2. **Mix dry sand/gravel** with shingles before grinding — acts as release agent
3. **Anti-stick discharge rollers** — on shingle-package grinders
4. **Process in cold weather** — RI winters are free anti-clog

### Screen Sizing
- Target 3/8" minus → use 3/4" round-hole screen (70% of output passes at half screen size)
- Target 1/2" minus → use 1" screen
- Hex-hole screens reduce plugging in gummy material
- Flip screens to extend life (use both sides)

## Creative Approaches

### Freeze-Grinding (Cryogenic)
- Established in rubber recycling — liquid nitrogen cools below glass transition temp
- Frozen asphalt shatters like glass instead of deforming
- Dry ice: ~$1–$2/lb retail. Would need significant quantity
- **Untested on shingles specifically, but physics is proven on similar materials**
- Potential for small-batch experimentation with dry ice + standard chipper

### RI Winter = Free Cryogenic Grinding
- **December–February in Rhode Island IS the cryogenic chamber**
- Stockpile warm-weather tear-offs → grind entire seasonal backlog in January
- Early morning on 15F days = maximum brittleness
- Even a low-powered chipper performs dramatically better in these conditions

## MVE Recommendation: 4 Options for 2–5 Tons/Job

### Option A: $500–$2,000 (Winter Only, Labor-Intensive)
- Used gas chipper/shredder (Bearcat-style, flail design)
- Operate below 40F only
- Magnetic plate for nail removal first
- Two passes + screen through 1/2" hardware cloth
- **20–50 hours per 5-ton job. Only if labor is free.**

### Option B: $10,000–$15,000 (Year-Round, Real Tool)
- **Used tub grinder** with 3/4" screen
- 1–5 tons/hour. Handles 5 tons in afternoon.
- Water spray on warm days
- **$1K–$3K/yr wear parts**
- **This is the real MVE for your scale.**

### Option C: $0 Capital (Service Model)
- Hire mobile grinding service or rent grinder for 1–2 days
- $500–$2,000 mobilization + per-ton for small jobs
- **Best for testing the model before equipment investment**

### Option D: $2,000–$5,000/Season (Hybrid — RECOMMENDED)
- **Stockpile** tear-offs from all warm-weather jobs
- **Rent** tub grinder or horizontal grinder for 1–2 days in January
- **Grind entire seasonal backlog** at peak cold (below 30F)
- Even a wood grinder works well on frozen shingles
- **Low capital, maximum throughput, learn before committing**
- **Upgrade to Option B when volume justifies ownership**
