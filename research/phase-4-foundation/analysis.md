## The Scale of the Problem

A 2,500 SF home with a full basement produces **160–190 tons of concrete.** That's 7–9 dump truck loads.

| Component | Volume | Weight |
|---|---|---|
| Foundation walls (poured, 8' × 8–10") | 35–45 yd³ | 70–90 tons |
| Basement slab (4" thick) | 30–32 yd³ | 60–64 tons |
| Footings (continuous, 20" × 10") | 10–12 yd³ | 20–24 tons |
| Garage slab (400 SF × 4") | 5–6 yd³ | 10–12 tons |
| Pier pads / column footings | 1–2 yd³ | 2–4 tons |
| **Total** | **~80–95 yd³** | **160–190 tons** |

**Conventional approach:** Excavator breaks it up → dump trucks → C&D facility → $15–$25/ton tipping. Cost: **$4,375–$6,775** per home. Material value recovered: $0.

**Our equipment:** Crane + Brokk. No excavator. The crane keeps moving. The Brokk does the precision work inside the hole.

---

## The Creative Solution: Section It, Lift It, Reuse It

Same philosophy as the roof, walls, and floor: **don't destroy it — section it and crane it out.**

### Saw-Cut Foundation Walls into Panels

A poured 8" foundation wall can be saw-cut into liftable panels — same concept as tilt-up concrete removal in reverse.

| Method | Cut Depth | Speed | Cost/LF | Clean Edge? |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Track-mounted wall saw | Up to 24" (single side) | 2–4 LF/min | $15–$35/LF | Excellent |
| Concrete chain saw (ICS, Husqvarna) | 8–16" | 3–5 LF/min | $5–$15/LF (self-perform) | Good |
| Walk-behind slab saw (for floor slab) | Up to 33" | 3–6 LF/min | $4.75–$7.50/LF | Excellent |

**Self-perform with a concrete chain saw ($3–$5K for the saw) is the MVE path.** An ICS 890F4 or Husqvarna K 6500 can plunge-cut 8" walls. One operator, no subcontractor.

### Panel Sizing — What the Crane Can Lift

Concrete = 100 lbs/SF for 8" walls. 50 lbs/SF for 4" slabs.

**Wall panels (8" thick):**

| Section Size | Weight | Crane Lift? |
|---|---|---|
| 4' × 4' | 1,600 lbs | Easy |
| 4' × 6' | 2,400 lbs | Easy |
| **4' × 8' (recommended)** | **3,200 lbs** | **Standard pick** |
| 8' × 8' (full height) | 6,400 lbs | Heavy — doable but less flexible for reuse |

**Slab sections (4" thick):**

| Section Size | Weight | Crane Lift? |
|---|---|---|
| **4' × 6' (recommended)** | **1,200 lbs** | Easy |
| 4' × 8' | 1,600 lbs | Easy |
| 6' × 8' | 2,400 lbs | Standard |

**Recommended: 4' × 8' wall panels (~3,200 lbs) and 4' × 6' slab sections (~1,200 lbs).** Liftable by crane, stackable on flatbed, reusable as landscape/retaining walls.

### Rigging for Concrete Panels

Core drill a 2" lifting hole near center of each panel. Rig with through-bolt and shackle, or use a Monkey Fist lift anchor system. Same rigging crew as roof/wall/floor phases.

## Crane + Brokk Workflow

```
SAW CREW (cuts)          BROKK (inside hole)         CRANE (lifts)
─────────────────        ─────────────────────        ─────────────────
Wall saw cuts →          Frees panels at base →       Lifts panels out →
Slab saw cuts →          Breaks loose sections →      Sets in staging →
                         Breaks footings →            Lifts footing chunks →
                         Cleans hole →                Lifts Brokk out (last)
```

### Day 1: Walls

| Step | Who | Duration |
|---|---|---|
| Saw-cut wall panels (verticals every 4', horizontal at footing) | Saw crew | 2–4 hrs |
| Core drill lifting holes | Saw crew | 1 hr |
| Brokk frees panels at base (breaks bond at footing joint) | Brokk operator | Ongoing |
| Crane lifts panels, sets in staging area | Crane + rigger | 15–20 min/panel |
| ~200 LF perimeter ÷ 4' panels = ~50 panels | | ~4–6 hrs crane time |

### Day 2: Slab + Footings

| Step | Who | Duration |
|---|---|---|
| Walk-behind slab saw cuts grid (4' × 6' sections) | Saw crew | 2–3 hrs |
| Core drill lifting holes in slab sections | Saw crew | 1 hr |
| Brokk loosens slab sections from subgrade | Brokk operator | Ongoing |
| Crane lifts slab sections out | Crane + rigger | ~3–4 hrs |
| Brokk breaks footings into chunks (most irregular — can't saw-cut below grade) | Brokk operator | 2–3 hrs |
| Crane lifts footing chunks into staging/dumpster | Crane | 1–2 hrs |
| Brokk with bucket cleans hole, final grading | Brokk operator | 1 hr |
| Crane lifts Brokk out of hole (last lift) | Crane | Last |

### Why This Combination Works

| Task | Crane Alone | Brokk Alone | Crane + Brokk |
|---|---|---|---|
| Lift 3,200 lb wall panels | Yes (needs pre-cutting) | No | **Brokk frees, crane lifts** |
| Work inside foundation hole | No (can't reach down) | Yes (fits through openings, remote) | **Complementary** |
| Break footings below grade | No | Yes (breaker attachment) | **Brokk breaks, crane lifts debris** |
| Precision near utilities | No | Yes (remote controlled) | **Brokk for precision** |
| Cut rebar | No | Yes (shear attachment) | **Brokk cuts, crane lifts bundles** |

### Broken Concrete Reuse (footings + damaged sections)

| Application | Tons Absorbed | Material Cost Avoided |
|---|---|---|
| New foundation base course (6–8" compacted) | 30–50 tons | $540–$1,500 |
| Driveway base | 15–25 tons | $270–$750 |
| Utility trench backfill | 5–15 tons | $90–$450 |
| Temp construction roads | 10–20 tons | $180–$600 |
| French drains / drainage layer | 5–10 tons | $90–$300 |

Sell to landscape supply yards at $8–$12/ton, or stockpile for next project. At worst, haul to Biszko or Narragansett Improvement ($12–$25/ton tipping) — but that's a fraction of the 175 tons you'd otherwise dump.

## Transition: Decon → Construction (ML Steel / Hybrid)

This is where Phase 4 meets the new build. The foundation hole is empty. What comes next depends on the structural system.

### Recompaction Test (between decon and construction)

| Item | What | Why |
|---|---|---|
| **Proof roll** | Loaded dump truck drives across subgrade, watch for deflection | Quick visual check — soft spots = more compaction needed |
| **Nuclear density test** | Gauge measures soil density in-place | Standard for residential — typically 95% modified Proctor required |
| **DCP (Dynamic Cone Penetrometer)** | Manual probe, measures bearing capacity by blow count | Cheaper than nuclear, good for spot-checking |
| **Cost** | $300–$800 per test (geotechnical sub) | One test per project, required before new foundation pour |

**After Brokk cleans the hole, before anything goes in:** Compact the subgrade, run a proof roll or DCP. The old foundation disturbed the soil — verify it can carry the new load. This is the bridge between decon and construction.

### ML Steel Foundation Options

| System | Description | Fit with Decon |
|---|---|---|
| **ML Steel on new poured foundation** | Standard — pour new walls/footings on compacted subgrade | Old RCA becomes base course under new footings |
| **ML Steel on helical piers** | Steel piers screwed into bearing soil, steel grade beams | Skips concrete entirely — fastest path from decon to structure |
| **Hybrid: old panels as forms + new pour** | Use intact wall panels as permanent exterior forms, pour against them | Old concrete = formwork + thermal mass. Reduce form cost. |
| **Hybrid: old panels as stem wall + steel above** | Stack/set old panels as stem wall, bolt ML Steel frame on top | Reuse at its most aggressive — old foundation IS the new foundation wall |

### The Hybrid Steel/Concrete Idea

If wall panels are cut clean and structurally sound (no cracking, rebar intact):

```
                ML STEEL FRAME
                ┌──────────────────────┐
                │  Bolted connections   │
                │  to sill plate/embed  │
     ═══════════╧══════════════════════╧═══════════
     │     OLD FOUNDATION WALL PANEL (reused)      │  ← Set by crane
     │     8" poured, rebar intact, saw-cut edges  │
     ═════════════════════════════════════════════
     │            NEW FOOTING (poured)             │  ← Minimal new concrete
     ═════════════════════════════════════════════
     │            COMPACTED SUBGRADE               │  ← RCA from old slab/footings
     ═════════════════════════════════════════════
```

**What this saves:**
- Forming cost for new foundation walls (~$8–$15/LF = $1,600–$3,000 for 200 LF)
- Concrete for new walls (~40 yd³ × $180 = $7,200)
- **Total potential savings: $8,800–$10,200** if old panels replace new foundation walls

**What needs engineering:** A PE stamps the reuse plan. Panel structural capacity, connection details (embed plates or through-bolts for ML Steel sill), waterproofing strategy. This is DfD in reverse — designing the new structure to accept old components.

### Site Work Sequence (Decon → Construction)

| Step | Description | Equipment |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Brokk cleans hole, final grading | Brokk + bucket |
| 2 | Crane lifts Brokk out | Crane |
| 3 | Subgrade compaction | Plate compactor or roller |
| 4 | Recompaction test (proof roll or DCP) | Geotech sub |
| 5 | Place RCA base course (old slab/footing rubble) | Crane + bucket or skid steer |
| 6 | Compact RCA base | Plate compactor |
| 7a | Pour new footings (if full new foundation) | Concrete truck |
| 7b | Set old wall panels on new footings (if hybrid) | Crane |
| 8 | ML Steel frame goes up | Crane |

**The crane never leaves the site.** It transitions from decon tool to construction tool. Same crew. Same machine. The gap between decon and construction is measured in days, not weeks.

## Permitting (RI)

| Requirement | Status |
|---|---|
| On-site concrete processing during permitted demo | **Allowed** — RIDEM Rule 11.3 |
| Reuse on same site (base, fill, landscape) | **No additional permit** |
