System Overview — How the Carriage Moves

Two independent systems work together: a guide rail constrains the carriage to a straight vertical path, and an Acme lead screw drives it up and down. The rail handles lateral loads (keeping the breaker aligned). The screw handles vertical motion and position hold.

Why C1045 Steel?

  • 0.45% carbon — harder than mild steel (C1018), better wear resistance under cyclic loading
  • Tensile strength: ~82,000 PSI (565 MPa)
  • Yield strength: ~60,000 PSI (414 MPa)
  • Ground and rolled threads = smooth surface finish, consistent pitch
  • Not stainless — will rust in wet environments. Acceptable for concrete demo (dry operation, oil film on threads from bronze nut wear)

Component 2: Guide System Track

The guide constrains the carriage to a single axis. It handles all lateral loads — keeping the breaker chisel aligned with the fracture line. This is a concrete demolition environment: silica dust, flying debris, sustained 1,400 BPM vibration. The guide system must survive all three.

Rejected: Polymer Pillow Blocks (igus drylin W)

The original design used igus drylin WS-20 aluminum rail with iglide J200 polymer-lined pillow blocks. While excellent for clean industrial automation, this system is wrong for concrete demolition:

  • Abrasive silica dust grinds polymer liners — concrete dust is essentially sandpaper
  • Sliding contact (not rolling) means dust gets trapped between liner and shaft, accelerating wear
  • 1,400 BPM vibration cycles the liner under load thousands of times per minute, compounding abrasive wear
  • Aluminum rail is softer than concrete aggregate — flying chunks can dent the running surface
  • Maintenance annoyance — liner replacement on-site between every job, maybe mid-job

The right guide system for this environment uses hardened steel on hardened steel, with sealed bearings protected inside the wheel body.

Selected: V-Groove Wheels on Steel V-Rail Winner

Why V-Groove Wins for Concrete Demo

  • Open geometry — dust, chips, and aggregate fall off the V-rail. Nothing to pack into. No enclosed bearing surface.
  • Hardened steel on hardened steel — V-rail is induction-hardened 1045 carbon steel or 400-series stainless. V-wheels have hardened outer races. Concrete dust can't grind either surface.
  • Sealed bearings inside the wheel — the rolling elements (balls or rollers) are inside the wheel body, behind seals. They never see the concrete dust. The dust only touches the wheel tread and the rail — both hardened steel.
  • Self-centering — the V-groove on the wheel mates with the V-profile on the rail. The carriage naturally tracks center. Misalignment tolerance is built in.
  • Proven in concrete cutting — the Hilti DST 10-CA wall saw uses hardened steel rollers on a steel rail track. Same principle. Same environment. Proven.
  • Zero maintenance — sealed bearings, no lubrication on running surfaces, no consumable liners
  • Vibration tolerant — rolling contact absorbs vibration better than sliding contact. Wheels can't "chatter" off the rail like a pillow block can.
  • Cheap DIY path — V-groove wheels on angle iron is a proven approach in CNC routers, rolling mills, and slide gates. Off-the-shelf at any industrial caster supplier.

How V-Wheels Mount to the Carriage

4 V-groove wheels (2 top, 2 bottom) ride on 2 parallel V-rails. The wider the spacing between top and bottom pairs, the more stable the carriage is against moment loads from breaker impact. Target: 12-18" vertical spacing between wheel pairs.
